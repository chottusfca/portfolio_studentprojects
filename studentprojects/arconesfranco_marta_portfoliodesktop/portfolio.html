Mighty Kiwi - A tale from 2019

“Mighty Kiwi” is also the name given to the clothing collection designed for the 2018-2019 Men’s Tennis Hawaii State Champion, Ryo Minkata.

“Mighty Kiwi” is also the nick name given to Ryo Minakata, the winner of this season’s Hawai’i Championship.

The player represented Hawai’i Preparatory Aacademy but he wanted a personal branded collection for the final.

The uniform was purchased by the player’s supporters and sold out before the start of the match.

The victory was featured in the local newspaper and on social media, featuring the winner, Ryo Minakata, wearing the clothing collection.

“Looking Past My Window” - 2020

The graphic novel “Looking Past My Window” explores the malleable topic of identity during the political turmoil and health crisis of the year 2020. This format allows for a fluid narration of a sensitive subject alleviated by soft visuals. The novel draws on the stylistic and plot narrations of the graphic novel “La Guardia” by Nnedi Okafor.

Dichotomy

“Dichotomy” is a collection of posters that conveys a series of visual contradictions. This effect is achieved by the exploration of capturing light in motion. This contradictive effect is emphasized by the mixing of photography and type that captures the essence of that dichotomy.

“The B stands for Boss”

These three icons represent the perpetuated war against women for their mere existence.

The B-girls were a group of women in the 70s prosecuted for accepting drinks men willingly purchased for them. The women were demonized by the media for “abusing their sex appeal” to trick men into buying them drinks.

Only the women were villainized, while none of the men who accepted the never-explicit sexual favors were blasted publicly in these same newspapers or even arrested for “paying” for sexual favors.

The three icons are a way for women to reclaim their public image by using their mugshots, an otherwise pathetic attempt at humiliating them, into a celebration of their femininity.

“Trust Me, It Gets Worse"

“Trust me, it gets worse” exposes a glimpse of the unfortunate reality of life as women, in some instances, as girls. This zine is a collection of a few of the most unfortunate cat call lines I can recall, starting with the earliest one at age 13. The booklet employs comedy as a tool to cope with trauma, but still confronts the fact that men consider women to be objects to their constant need for validation and attention.
